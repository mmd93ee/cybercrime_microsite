Global Landscape of Cybersecurity

Cybersecurity is not a technical problem.
It never was.

We keep building better locks while leaving the windows open. The persistent gap between cybersecurity investment and cybersecurity outcomes is, at its core, a human problem — not an engineering one.

When we discuss cybersecurity, the conversation almost inevitably turns to tools: firewalls, anti-malware, encryption protocols, vulnerability scanning. The language is technical, the vendors are technical, and the job adverts are technical. And yet year after year, the overwhelming majority of successful attacks bypass all of this — because they target people, not systems.

A 2015 IBM study found that 95% of security incidents involve human error as a contributing factor. The UK's 2020 Cyber Security Breaches Survey found that 86% of businesses identified phishing as their most disruptive attack type. You do not need to break encryption if you can simply persuade someone to hand over their credentials.

This is not an argument against technical controls. Firewalls matter. Patch management matters. Multi-factor authentication matters enormously. The argument is that these controls exist within a social system — and a social system will always route around frictions it perceives as unnecessary or burdensome. A security control that people won't use is not a security control. It is theatre.

The usability trap

The National Cyber Security Centre discovered something counterintuitive when it revised its password guidance. For years, conventional wisdom demanded complexity: mixed case, special characters, regular rotation. Organisations complied. Users, faced with unmemorable passwords across dozens of systems, responded rationally: they wrote them on sticky notes, reused them everywhere, or chose predictable patterns that technically met the rules. The security benefit was negligible.

The revised guidance — use three random words, make it long, save it in your browser — was simpler. It was also more secure, precisely because people would actually follow it. This is the socio-technical lesson in its purest form.

When security becomes harder to work within, we build strategies to avoid compliance — and our behaviour is driven not just by the perception of threat, but by the perceived personal cost of managing it.

Research by Sadok, Alter and Bednar (2020) found that employees in SMEs frequently had to breach security policy simply to do their jobs effectively. The policies had been designed in isolation from actual working practice. The result was not a more secure organisation — it was one where security policy had been quietly discarded, creating invisible vulnerabilities.

The national picture

What is striking about the UK's National Cyber Security Strategy (2016–2021) is how much of it is about people. The three pillars — Defend, Deter, Develop — are ostensibly about national capability, but the substance of each is overwhelmingly human. Defend means building individual and organisational awareness. Develop means investing in talent and education. The state is positioned simultaneously as teacher, soldier, and enabler.

Johnston et al.'s (2018) finding that security compliance is fundamentally about communication — tailored to the specific psychological context of the audience — is the honest conclusion. Security is persuasion. The most effective national cyber strategies are not the ones that spend the most on technical infrastructure. They are the ones that best understand that technology and behaviour are inseparable.

Internet Governance & Disinformation

Information has become the weapon of choice for geopolitical conflict.

The era of superpower military confrontation may be waning. In its place: coordinated campaigns of disinformation that destabilise democracies without a single soldier crossing a border — and a global governance framework wholly unprepared to respond.

We are living through the longest period of sustained peace between world superpowers in five centuries. Roser's (2016) conflict data shows deaths from large inter-state wars have declined dramatically since World War II. But conflict has not disappeared — it has changed form. And the internet has given it a new, extraordinarily powerful vector.

Russia has been running disinformation operations since at least 1921. What has changed is scale, speed, and cost. Where disinformation once required intelligence agents, printing presses, and patient infiltration of foreign media ecosystems, it now requires a laptop, social media accounts, and a strategy for gaming recommendation algorithms. The tools of propaganda have been democratised — and seized by states who understand their potential.

What disinformation actually does

The goal of disinformation is not primarily to make people believe false things. Research by Levinger (2018) suggests the real objective is more insidious: to undermine the audience's confidence that anything is true. A population uncertain of what to believe is less able to organise, less able to hold institutions accountable, and more susceptible to authoritarian actors who claim to offer certainty.

The White Helmets case illustrates this precisely. Russian-linked Twitter accounts — many posting over 150 times daily — coordinated around key news events to inject counter-narratives. When the White Helmets filmed a mannequin challenge, the footage was stripped of context, reposted as evidence of staging, picked up by Russian state media, and broadcast as a question mark over every piece of documentation the group had ever produced. The objective was not to prove a falsehood. It was to make truth feel uncertain.

Influencing the marketplace where ideational contestation occurs can be highly impactful in countries whose democracy is designed such that political processes interact with social discourse — shaping that discourse shapes national direction.

Whyte & Mazanec, Understanding Cyber Warfare (2018)

Why governance models fail

Glen's (2014) three governance models — Open Multistakeholderism, Repressive Multilateral, and Open Multilateral — were developed to address infrastructure governance. The fundamental problem when applied to disinformation is that they describe who controls the pipes, not what flows through them. Content moderation, as the data on democracy type versus internet freedom clearly shows, is almost perfectly correlated with political system. Full democracies operate free internets. Authoritarian states operate controlled ones. The divergence is not a policy failure — it reflects incompatible values about what the internet is for.

Meanwhile, the infrastructure through which disinformation actually flows is controlled by a handful of US companies. Facebook, YouTube, and Instagram dominate global social media outside China. In 2019, disinformation campaigns were active on Facebook in 56 countries. Facebook spent $19.7 million lobbying the US government on content policy in 2020. A global multistakeholder body capable of moderating this environment is not a realistic near-term prospect.

What actually works

The French example is instructive. Russia's 2018 campaign against the French presidential election — one of the largest disinformation operations since 2016 — failed. The reasons are telling: Google provided proactive fact-checking, mainstream media developed reliability protocols, and the French population had cultivated broad scepticism toward online content. The response was not a new governance framework. It was a combination of commercial action, civil society resilience, and media literacy. A socio-technical response to a socio-technical threat — consistent with the pattern that emerges across every domain of cybercrime research.

Organised Immigration Crime

Removing online content from illegal migration routes may put more lives at risk.

UK government policy treats online content advertising illegal migration as criminal infrastructure to be removed. The research suggests it may also be the thing keeping migrants alive — and we have very little evidence either way.

The instinct is understandable. Over 9,500 people crossed the English Channel in small boats in 2020 — a four-fold increase on 2019. Approximately 300 deaths have been recorded in that crossing since 1999. The human cost is real. And content on social media does facilitate the process: advertising smuggling services, sharing route information, connecting potential migrants with networks that can help them move.

The UK government's response has been to pressure social media companies to remove it. The National Crime Agency requested 1,200 account removals in 2020; 700 were actioned. The Home Secretary wrote directly to major platform CEOs. The framing is consistent: this content is criminal infrastructure. The problem is that the research does not support this framing — at least not cleanly.

Technology as a protective factor

Diba, Papanicolaou, and Antonopoulos's 2019 study — one of the few to examine the role of digital technology in UK-bound smuggling — found something important: information and communication technologies appear to empower migrants. Evidence showed migrants using online content to identify and avoid predatory smuggling groups, to assess routes, and to gather realistic information about journeys.

Zhang et al. (2018) challenged the dominant predator-victim narrative, finding that the smuggler-migrant relationship more closely resembles a business transaction, with migrants actively vetting, negotiating, and managing their own risk. Campana and Gelsthorpe (2020) describe migrants researching smugglers online before meeting them in person — combining digital intelligence with personal verification. This is not passive victimhood. It is constrained rational decision-making.

We do not know whether online content is primarily a shop window for organised crime — or a resource that makes dangerous journeys marginally less dangerous. The policy assumes the former. The evidence suggests it may be the latter.

The evidence gap

This creates an acute policy dilemma with life-and-death stakes. If content is removed and migrants lose access to information that helped them identify exploitation or assess genuine risks, what actually happens? The UN's report for the Global Compact for Migration found that many migrants are not well-informed about the costs and risks of illegal migration before beginning journeys — and Tjaden (2020) shows that factual knowledge is not, in any case, a reliable predictor of whether someone decides to migrate. The drivers are structural: persecution, poverty, family separation. Awareness campaigns are largely ineffective at deterrence.

What makes this particularly urgent is the quality of the evidence base. The primary UK study on this topic — Diba et al. (2019) — conducted 18 interviews, half of which were with law enforcement. No UK migrants were included in the sample at all. The conclusion that technology empowers migrants cannot be adequately validated without hearing from migrants themselves. Yet policy interventions affecting thousands of lives are proceeding without that evidence.

Cybercrime — Critical Perspectives

Cybercrime is not old wine in new bottles.
It is new wine entirely.

In 2001, Peter Grabosky argued that cybercrime was simply traditional crime using a new medium. Two decades of evidence have not supported that claim — and the failure of the justice system to respond adequately to cybercrime is one of its consequences.

Grabosky's argument was intuitive. Fraud is fraud whether it happens in person or by email. Theft is theft whether a wallet is picked or a bank account drained. The motives — greed, power, revenge — are ancient. Why should the internet require us to rethink fundamental criminological categories?

The problem is that this view treats the internet as a channel — a pipe through which existing behaviours flow — rather than as a social environment with its own logic. And the evidence that cyberspace constitutes a genuinely distinct social reality is now substantial.

Cyberspace as a social ontology

Drawing on Searle's (1995) theory of social reality, it is possible to argue that cyberspace has its own ontological structure — arising from physical infrastructure but not reducible to it. The word 'friend' means something different on social media than in the physical world. Wilcox and Stephen (2013) found measurable behavioural differences between online and offline social relationships, including reduced self-restraint and a tendency toward self-promotion that would be considered socially unacceptable face-to-face. If the social environment is genuinely different, then the behaviours it generates will be structured differently too.

Where classical theory breaks down

Routine Activity Theory (Cohen & Felson, 1979) requires that a motivated offender, a suitable target, and a lack of capable guardianship converge in the same space and time. In cyberspace there is no shared physical space. A ransomware operator in Eastern Europe can attack a hospital in Manchester without leaving their apartment. The theory can be salvaged only if we accept that 'space' in cyberspace means something fundamentally different to physical space — which is itself an admission that the theory needs modification.

Wilhelm (2020) found that de-territorialisation, asynchronicity, and anonymity all foster the normalisation of cyber-deviance — making it far easier for offenders to neutralise their own behaviour and perceive their victims as abstract or absent.

Techniques of Neutralisation (Sykes & Matza, 1957) fare better — but again with modification. The psychological distance that cyberspace creates between offender and victim makes neutralisation techniques dramatically more available. Research on online flaming by Hwang et al. (2016) found that people's perceptions of acceptable behaviour differ markedly online — partly because the social cues that ordinarily regulate behaviour in physical environments are absent.

The justice gap this creates

The practical consequence of treating cybercrime as equivalent to physical crime is measurable and serious. In 2010, robbing a US bank netted an average of $7,643 — and carried a high probability of a lengthy prison sentence. In 2011, online fraud cost US victims an average of $3,666 per incident, totalling $1.1 billion. Those caught were unlikely to face jail; when they did, sentences were short. The asymmetry of risk and reward in cybercrime is not incidental. It reflects a justice system that has not caught up with a distinct category of offending that requires distinct responses.

The UK's Online Safety Bill represents the first serious attempt to create a regulatory regime for harmful online content — but its contested definition of 'legal but harmful' illustrates precisely how difficult it is to establish shared social norms in a space where the rules of community are still being negotiated. Cybercrime will remain a distinct category of offending until the law, criminological theory, and social norms converge around a common framework for digital society. That convergence has not yet arrived.

Cyber Espionage

Espionage at industrial scale —
with near-zero attribution risk.

Cyber espionage has transformed intelligence gathering from a high-risk, labour-intensive operation into something that can be conducted remotely, cheaply, and at continental scale. The institutional response has not kept pace.

Consider a single year's activity: in January 2021, hackers linked to Hezbollah breached telecoms companies across the US, UK, Egypt, Israel, Lebanon, Jordan, Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and the Palestinian Authority — simultaneously. In March, Chinese government hackers exploited Microsoft Exchange vulnerabilities to access the data of over 30,000 organisations globally. In April, MI5 warned that over 10,000 UK professionals had been targeted by hostile states via LinkedIn over the preceding five years.

This is not exceptional activity. This is a routine year.

Why attribution is so difficult

Attribution in cyber operations is deceptively complex. It requires identifying the machine, the individual operating it, and the organisation directing them — and maintaining sufficient confidence in each layer to justify action. At every step, sophisticated actors introduce doubt: proxies, false flags, shared toolkits used by multiple threat groups, and operations run through criminal intermediaries who provide state sponsors with plausible deniability.

The structural problem identified by Lin (2019) is that the evidential threshold required for public attribution — sufficient to justify an accusation or legal response — is almost always higher than the operational threshold at which intelligence agencies are privately confident. States frequently know who attacked them. They cannot prove it to the standard international action requires.

Cyber espionage allows a hostile actor to steal information remotely, cheaply, and on an industrial scale — with relatively little risk to its intelligence officers. It is traditional espionage with the risk profile of commercial software delivery.

MI5, UK Security Service Briefing

Two categories of espionage

Kremling and Parker (2017) distinguish between economic and political cyber espionage. Economic espionage — the theft of trade secrets, IP, and R&D data to accelerate domestic industrial development — was estimated by Detica (2011) to cost the UK £16.8 billion annually when combined with industrial espionage impacts. China has been the most consistent practitioner, and the scale is not incidental: it represents a deliberate strategy to compress the innovation timeline for priority domestic industries, from semiconductors to pharmaceuticals.

Political espionage is broader: collecting intelligence on adversary governments, military capabilities, and diplomatic positions. Russia, China, Iran, and North Korea all operate large, sophisticated cyber intelligence operations whose targets extend far beyond government systems into media organisations, think tanks, legal firms, and academic institutions. In the months before Russia's invasion of Ukraine, cyber espionage operations intensified sharply across NATO member states.

The limits of the national response

The UK's NCSC, established in 2016 as part of a five-year National Cyber Security Strategy, has become a genuine model for other nations. The ITU ranked the UK second globally on its Cybersecurity Index. But the structural limitations on countering state-sponsored espionage remain formidable. Domestic law ends at the border. International law contains no specific prohibition on espionage — only arms control regimes and diplomatic conventions that rely entirely on voluntary compliance. And the states most active in cyber espionage are precisely those with the most leverage to block international agreements that would constrain them.

Regulating Power Conflicts in Cyberspace

Code is law.
But whose code — and whose law?

Lawrence Lessig argued that cyberspace is regulated not just by law, but by architecture, markets, and social norms — and that this made it more governable, not less. The UK Online Safety Bill and the Chinese internet offer two very different demonstrations of what that looks like at scale.

The early internet was supposed to be ungovernable. John Perry Barlow's 1996 Declaration of the Independence of Cyberspace proclaimed that governments had no sovereignty over the digital world. Lessig's response was patient and precise: the internet is already regulated — by its code. The question is not whether cyberspace will be governed, but who writes the rules, and in whose interest.

Lessig's four modalities of constraint — Law, Social Norms, Markets, and Architecture — interact to shape the behaviour of what he calls the 'pathetic dot': the individual user subject to forces they rarely perceive and almost never control. Law makes behaviours illegal. Social norms make them unacceptable. Markets make them expensive or unavailable. Architecture makes them technically impossible.

The Online Safety Bill as a test case

The UK Online Safety Bill (2022) deploys all four modalities simultaneously and explicitly. It creates new criminal offences, including cyber-flashing — which affects approximately three-quarters of girls aged 12–18 who have received unsolicited intimate images. It establishes a statutory duty of care making platforms legally responsible for user safety. It creates an independent regulator with audit powers. And — most significantly for Lessig's theory — it requires platforms to implement technical solutions at scale, effectively compelling them to code regulation into their products.

This last point is crucial. A law saying 'do not expose children to pornography' requires individual enforcement. A technical requirement that all users verify their age before accessing adult content regulates at the population level through architecture. Lessig called this 'regulating code to regulate better' — and the Bill's age verification provisions are a direct application of that principle.

When Meta's platforms were found to account for over half of all UK online grooming offences, parliamentary scrutiny found that child safety was structurally secondary to profit. Market failure — not moral failure — made legislation necessary.

China: the modalities at their logical extreme

If the Online Safety Bill represents a liberal democracy wrestling with the tension between freedom and safety, China represents the abolition of that tension by design. All three government-owned internet service providers, all 374 television channels, and all official print media are state controlled. The Cyberspace Administration of China operates under direct oversight of President Xi Jinping. The Great Firewall blocks Facebook, YouTube, Twitter, and Google Play by default.

What is striking is that this has not reduced adoption. WeChat has the same number of domestic users as Instagram has globally. Chinese users spend almost two hours per day on social media. A 2008 survey found that 80% of Chinese internet users believed content should be managed — and 85% believed government should be responsible for doing so. The panopticon effect — where compliance is achieved by making people believe observation is always possible — may be doing most of the regulatory work.

The implications are uncomfortable. The same architecture that can protect children from harmful content in the UK can silence dissidents in Xinjiang. The code is technically neutral. The values written into it are not. As Freedom House (2022) finds, 64% of the world's internet users are now subject to some form of government content control. The question of who regulates the internet, and according to whose values, is not an abstract governance question. It is the central political question of the digital age.

  • Law — makes behaviours illegal, creates deterrence through prosecution risk
  • Social Norms — makes behaviours culturally unacceptable within a community
  • Markets — makes behaviours expensive, unavailable, or commercially unviable
  • Architecture — makes behaviours technically impossible by design

Cybercrime-as-a-Service: Regulating and Disrupting the Criminal Business Model

How organised crime adopted the SaaS playbook — and why the regulatory response has so far failed to match it.

The industrialisation of crime

The 'as a service' model — originally a legitimate cloud computing innovation — has been adopted wholesale by organised criminal enterprises. What started with Amazon Web Services in 2002 as a way of delivering software with lower cost and no on-premises infrastructure has become the operational template for distributing ransomware, denial-of-service capabilities, deepfake generation tools, espionage services, and exploit kits.

The logic is identical to legitimate SaaS. A ransomware-as-a-service operator maintains the technical infrastructure, handles payment processing in cryptocurrency, manages victim negotiations, and takes a commission — typically 20–30% of ransom receipts. Affiliates, who need no technical sophistication, gain access to capabilities that would previously have required years of expertise to develop. The barrier to entry for committing serious cybercrime has effectively collapsed.

The cybercriminal is no longer a counterculture figure in a basement. They are a highly motivated, commercially organised actor operating within a market structure that mirrors — and in some respects outperforms — the legitimate software industry.

The DarkSide model

The 2021 attack on the Colonial Pipeline — which disrupted one third of US petroleum product consumption for three weeks — was carried out by DarkSide, a group that had existed for less than nine months. In that time, they had evolved from a team with tools into a structured affiliate programme targeting organisations in 15 countries. DarkSide's operation included ransom note templates, cryptocurrency payment portals, victim management systems, a PR function for managing media coverage of attacks, and a dedicated negotiation service. This is not a loose criminal gang. It is a technology company with a product, a customer base, and a support operation — that happened to be extorting critical national infrastructure.

Why the regulatory response is failing

The UK's primary legal instrument against cybercrime remains the Computer Misuse Act 1990 — legislation written before the World Wide Web existed. It has been amended incrementally and is increasingly strained by an ecosystem its drafters could not have imagined. Crucially, the Act focuses on offences against computer systems; it does not address the value chain that makes those offences possible and profitable: the access brokers, the exploit kit developers, the cryptocurrency tumblers, the dark web marketplaces.

Internationally, the Budapest Convention on Cybercrime (2001) is the only multilateral treaty in force. It has no enforcement mechanism. Implementation into domestic law is inconsistent. And the states most active in the cybercrime ecosystem — notably Russia and China — are either not signatories or are actively undermining it. Russia's REvil ransomware gang was significantly disrupted in late 2021 following US diplomatic pressure. By April 2022, following Russia's invasion of Ukraine and the breakdown in US-Russia communications, the group had reconstituted itself. The January 2022 arrests of lower-level gang members were, as subsequent analysis confirmed, political theatre.

Deepfakes and the erosion of reality

Deepfakes represent a distinct dimension of the cybercrime-as-a-service ecosystem: not primarily extortion, but harm. Europol (2022) found that 96% of deepfake content online is non-consensual sexual material, and 90% of that targets women. The technology enabling a film studio to de-age an actor is the same technology being used to create synthetic sexual images of private individuals without their knowledge or consent — and distributing them for entertainment, coercion, or revenge.

The societal harm extends beyond individual victims. Vaccari and Chadwick's (2020) research found that even when people correctly identify footage as fake, the experience of uncertainty persistently reduces trust in all digital media. A population that cannot trust what it sees is less capable of democratic deliberation. This is not a privacy problem. It is a threat to the epistemological foundations of open societies.

Key Finding
Criminal ecosystems adapt faster than legislators
The average ransomware gang operates for 17 months before shutdown or rebrand — far less than the average legislative cycle. A regulatory framework that responds to threats after they have already evolved is structurally unable to keep pace. This makes investment in market-based responses — cyber security as a sector, insurance requirements, and protective technology — at least as important as legislative reform.
